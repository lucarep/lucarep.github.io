INTRO⌗

In statistics, the kth order statistic of a statistical sample is equal to its kth-smallest value. Together with rank statistics, order statistics are among the most fundamental tools in non-parametric statistics and inference.

ALL VALUES ARE IMPORTANT, BUT SOME SOME VALUES ARE MORE IMPORTANT THAN OTHERS⌗

Yes, I’m not quoting George Orwell at random, the reason is that in order statistics, there are much more important numbers than others within the dataset. These values are the minimum, the maximum, the one in the middle(which in this case also represents the median)and other quantiles of the sample.

UNIFORM DISTRIBUTION⌗

When using probability theory to analyze order statistics of random samples from a continuous distribution, the cumulative distribution function is used to reduce the analysis to the case of order statistics of the uniform distribution.

AN EXAMPLE⌗

The order statistic as I see it is easier to understand with an example, so let’s look at one. Imagine that you have a sample consisting of four elements, and suppose that these are their measured values: $$6,9,3,8$$ Well, what we need to do now to get an order statistic, is trivially put them in order.

What we get then is: $$3,6,8,9$$ The order statistic is then denoted as: $$x_{(1)} = 3, x_{(2)} = 6, x_{(3)} = 8, x_{(4)} = 9$$ Where, the first order statistic (or smallest order statistic) is always the minimum of the sample: $$X_{(1)} = min(X_{1},…,X_{n}) $$ Similarly, for a sample of size n, the nth order statistic (or largest order statistic) is the maximum, that is: $$X_{(n)} = max(X_{1},…,X_{n}) $$ Very interesting, is also the sample range defined as the difference between the maximum and minimum. It is a function of the order statistics: $$\text{Range}(X_{1},…,X_{n}) = X_{(n)} - X_{(1)}$$

PROBABILITY DENSITY⌗

The PDF, by definition, for X(k) is: $$\lim_{dx \to 0} \frac{p(X_{(1)}\in(x,x + dx))}{dx}$$
